Chapter 11: Subjective Dimensions of Heaviness

How personal history and context shape heavy listening.

Navigate Chapters

Moving past technical implementation, this section examines how heaviness functions as a perceptual experience: one that varies between listeners based on their musical background, listening environment, and cultural context. The eight producers’ distinct interpretations of ‘In Solitude’ clearly demonstrate heaviness as a subjective rather than purely objective quality.

Beyond Technical Definitions

Despite identifying common technical elements that contribute to heaviness, our research suggested that listeners do not always agree on which production approach sounds “heaviest”. Some find maximum impact in hyperreal techniques with perfect alignment and enhanced clarity and sonic weight, while others connect more with the organic power of naturalistic approaches that preserve performance energy. This divergence is not about right or wrong; it is about different pathways to creating powerful musical experiences. Dave Otero described heaviness as ‘that thing that washes over you and makes you feel such undeniable weight’, which highlights how it operates as an emotional and physical experience rather than just a technical achievement.

The Perception Framework

Our perception of heaviness is influenced by several factors, such as:

  • Listening History: Previous exposure to metal creates reference points that shape expectations.
  • Listening Environment: The same mix sounds remarkably different through headphones versus speakers, in a car versus a quiet room, or at different volume levels. Certain production approaches excel in specific environments; hyperreal productions often shine in headphone listening, while naturalistic approaches may better translate to physical spaces.
  • Evolving Standards: What once sounded impossibly heavy becomes normalized over time. Andrew Scheps noted that heaviness often comes from novelty: 'You get used to something, and it’s no longer heavy... When you hear it the tenth time, it’s not as heavy’. This explains metal’s constant evolution toward greater extremity.

The perception of heaviness is not limited to purely sonic elements but also includes visual and contextual dimensions. Album artwork, band image, and cultural associations all contribute to how we experience heaviness.

The Personal History Effect

Our individual musical journeys significantly influence our perception of heaviness. A listener raised on 1980s thrash might find Nordström's production style authentically heavy, while someone who discovered metal through modern djent might connect more deeply with Odeholm's hyperreal approach. Even within the same subgenre, our formative listening experiences, such as which albums introduced us to metal or which bands defined "heaviness" during our most impressionable years, create powerful reference points that colour all future listening experiences.

Building Your Own Heaviness Reference Library

Recognising heaviness as a subjective experience does not mean abandoning standards; it means making them explicit. One practical way to do this is to build your own reference library of 'heavy for me' tracks across eras and subgenres.

Start by choosing recordings that once felt overwhelmingly heavy when you first encountered them – not only current favourites, but also albums that defined heaviness at different points in your listening life. Include both more naturalistic, 'band in a room' productions and more hyperreal, engineered examples.

For each track, ask yourself:

  • What makes this feel heavy to me – composition, performance, production, or a particular combination?
  • Where would I place it on the Naturalistic–Hyperreal spectrum introduced earlier in this guide?
  • In which listening situations (headphones, car, small speakers, live-level playback) does this mix feel heaviest?

Over time, this curated library becomes a personal calibration tool. Returning to it while you mix helps you anchor decisions about tone, dynamics, and balance in a set of references that are historically and emotionally meaningful to you, rather than in abstract ideals.

Balancing Act

The most effective approaches to creating heaviness recognize and work with these subjective dimensions. Rather than pursuing technical ideals in isolation, producers balance several perceptual trade-offs:

  • Clarity vs. Density: Finding the right balance between clear definition and overwhelming sonic mass.
  • Precision vs. Expression: Determining how much human feel should remain versus engineered perfection.
  • Modern vs. Traditional: Connecting to metal’s sonic heritage or pushing toward new sonic possibilities.

Understanding the subjective nature of heaviness does not diminish its importance; it enriches our appreciation of the craft involved in creating it. The diversity of approaches demonstrated in the ‘In Solitude’ experiment shows multiple valid paths to creating heavy productions that resonate with different listeners in different contexts.

Call to Action

Conduct your own subjective heaviness experiment by reflecting on your personal metal journey while listening to the eight mixes of 'In Solitude'. Consider which approaches resonate most with your instinctive sense of heaviness and how this might connect to your listening biography. As you do so, begin a playlist or folder of ‘heavy for me’ tracks, including these mixes and key albums from different phases of your listening history, and note where you would place each one on the Naturalistic–Hyperreal spectrum.

Then share the mixes with friends whose metal backgrounds differ from yours: perhaps someone who entered through a different subgenre or generation of metal. Compare their impressions with your own and add contrasting choices to your reference library. Over time, revisiting this collection will help you calibrate both your ears and your production decisions against a consciously articulated sense of what heaviness means to you

Key Heuristics

  • Recognise that your sense of "heavy" is shaped by listening history, subgenre socialisation, and playback systems; it is not universal.
  • Test mixes on multiple systems and with different listeners to see how stable your intended heaviness really is.
  • Be aware of perceptual adaptation: constant exposure to extreme productions can make you over-process; take breaks and reset your ears.
  • Use reference tracks consciously, asking "heavy for whom, in which scene, and on what systems?" rather than assuming one-size-fits-all standards.
